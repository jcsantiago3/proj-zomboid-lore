WENDY WALKER — FINAL DIARY ENTRY 04

WAREHOUSE INCIDENT

Tonight was the night Carl had told me Bob will be into the warehouse. I waited for the guard rotation before going in.

I entered through a second-floor window and made my way down inside.

He was there when I reached the lower level.

At first I stopped moving. I thought I had made a mistake.

But it didn’t change when I got closer.

He was standing. He was moving. But nothing about it felt like him anymore.

His skin had turned ashen gray, dull and lifeless, like something already decaying.

This was not the man I remembered.

Whatever the facility had done, it had changed him.

I said his name.

No response.

I opened the cage.

I remembered Carl gave me a syringe earlier and told me it would help when I saw Bob.

I used it because I believed him.

I didn't know what it was meant to do. But it changed something in him.

Not into something better.

Into something unstable.

Like whatever was holding him together stopped holding at all.

His movements became erratic. His breathing sharpened.

The calm, gentle and awareness he once had was gone.

In its place was something unstable. . . agitated, unpredictable, and increasingly aggressive.

I pushed him away and grabbed a bat nearby just to get distance.

I swung wildly because I didn’t know how else to stop myself from staying there.

I hesitated... I wouldn't want to hurt Bob.

That’s when he bit me.

. . . The syringe did not restore him.

. . . It pushed him further away.

Bob was still on my arm and I can't let go.

That's when I noticed a piece of paper in Bob's pocket.

I picked it up

I saw that it was a drawing our son Johnny drew for Bob. It was Bob standing like a hero, with the words, “Dad will save the world.”

I held it up, holding tears. And showed the drawing towards Bob.

Bob froze.

His aggression stopped and his grip loosened.

The drawing seemed to anchor him, if only briefly—some fragment of memory or instinct holding him back.

That was my chance. I ran out of the warehouse after that.

As I ran off, I heard Bob. Bob’s behavior seemingly snapped back. . . erratic, unstable.

I looked back, wanting to say goodbye to the man I once loved. But I saw it. . . He grabbed the bat and, in a crude imitation of memory, held it with intent, as if recalling how it was used.

I alerted the military and remaining staff.

Bob is still inside that place. But I don’t think the person I knew is inside it anymore.

I keep trying to hold onto his name when I think about him. It doesn’t feel like it belongs to what I saw.

To whoever is reading this, please know that I am trying my hardest to keep myself sane. I think. . . This is the end. . .

I'm sorry, Bob.

I'm sorry, Johnny.

- Wendy
